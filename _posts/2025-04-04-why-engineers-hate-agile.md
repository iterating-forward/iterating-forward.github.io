I've yet to meet a developer who says, "You know what I love? Sprint planning."

## The Real Problem

Developers don't hate Agile - they hate fake Agile. The version where they:

- Estimate tickets they didn't write
- Attend retrospectives that change nothing
- Get interrupted every 2 hours for a "quick sync"

Real Agile should increase flow time, not fragment it. If your implementation of Scrum makes coding harder, you're doing it wrong.

## What Developers Actually Want

I surveyed a dozen senior engineers last month. Not a scientific study, just conversations. Every single one said some version of the same thing: "Let me do my job."

**Uninterrupted time:**

The research on deep work isn't new. Context switching kills productivity. A single Slack ping can cost 23 minutes of recovery time. Developers know this intuitively because they feel it every day.

Yet the average Scrum team has standup (15 min), refinement (1 hr), planning (2 hrs), review (1 hr), retro (1 hr), plus ad-hoc syncs. That's six hours of scheduled interruptions per sprint, minimum. More if your Scrum Master believes in "collaboration."

AI tools made this worse, not better. Now developers can write code faster in their limited focus time, which makes the interruptions feel even more costly. You can bang out a feature in two hours if nobody bothers you. You can spend all day trying if people keep pinging.

**Context over tickets:**

The Jira ticket says "implement X." The developer asks "why?" The PM says "because the stakeholder asked for it." The developer asks why again. Nobody knows.

Developers want to understand the problem they're solving. Not because they're difficult. Because understanding the problem helps them build better solutions. A developer who knows the user's pain point will make different choices than one who's just implementing a spec.

Good Agile teams give context. Bad ones give instructions.

**Trust:**

The standup exists because someone doesn't trust the team to communicate problems without a daily check-in. The ticket estimates exist because someone doesn't trust developers to manage their own time. The velocity tracking exists because someone needs proof that work is happening.

High-trust teams need less process. They talk when something's blocked. They estimate when it helps, not because the chart requires it. They skip the ceremonies that don't serve them.

Engineers don't hate Agile values. They hate the surveillance dressed up as process.

## The Contrarian Take

The best engineering teams I've seen have fewer Scrum ceremonies, not more. They've earned the right through trust and delivery.
