**Summary**: Platform engineering becomes the hottest trend in DevOps. But strip away the buzzwords, and it's just removing impediments - the thing Scrum Masters should have been doing all along.

**Tech Context (Jul 2025)**: Gartner predicts 80% of software engineering orgs will have platform teams by 2026. 55%+ already adopted platform engineering. Google reports 92% of CIOs planning AI integrations into platforms.

---

"We're building an Internal Developer Platform" is the new "We're doing Agile."

## The Core Insight

Platform engineering's core insight - that developers waste hours on tooling friction - is exactly what a Scrum Master should be escalating as an impediment.

High-maturity platform teams report 40-50% reductions in cognitive load for developers.

75% of developers lose over six hours weekly due to tool fragmentation.

## Why This Became a Separate Discipline

Because Scrum Masters kept losing the argument.

I've sat in dozens of meetings where a Scrum Master escalated "deployment takes too long" or "our CI pipeline breaks twice a week" or "developers spend half their time fighting tooling." The response was always some version of: "That's an infrastructure problem, not a team problem. We'll put it on the backlog."

It stayed on the backlog. For years.

Platform engineering got executive buy-in because it reframed the same problem in language leadership understood: developer productivity metrics. Time to first commit. Deployment frequency. Mean time to recovery.

Suddenly the same impediments Scrum Masters had been screaming about became strategic initiatives with budget lines.

**The uncomfortable parallel:**

A platform team's job is to remove friction from developer workflows. A Scrum Master's job is to remove impediments from the team.

These are the same job.

The difference is organizational power. Platform teams get headcount, roadmaps, and KPIs. Scrum Masters get a Confluence page and good intentions.

**What this should teach us:**

If you're a Scrum Master in an org with a platform team, work with them. The impediments you can't fix are the problems they're funded to solve.

If you're in an org without a platform team, document every hour your developers lose to tooling friction. Build the business case. Use the platform engineering research. Gartner says 80% of orgs will have platform teams by 2026. Get ahead of it.

The companies that figured this out early have developers who ship instead of fight their tools. The companies that didn't have developers writing "environment setup" tickets in perpetuity.

## The Contrarian Take

Every impediment your team has about infrastructure, tooling, or deployment is a platform engineering problem. If you've been escalating these for years with no result, you understand why platform engineering needed to exist.
