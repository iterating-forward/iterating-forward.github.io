**Summary**: After months of watching teams generate retro action items that never happen, it's time to admit the systemic reasons why.

**Tech Context (Oct 2025)**: Amazon announces largest-ever layoff round (14,000 corporate roles). Teams under pressure deprioritize improvement work. Gartner reports on platform engineering reducing cognitive load.

---

I audited a team's last 12 retrospectives. They had 47 action items. They completed 3.

## The Systemic Problem

Retrospective action items don't get done because they compete with sprint work, and sprint work always wins.

The system is designed for delivery, not improvement.

If your team can't allocate capacity for improvement work without falling behind on commitments, that's not a discipline problem - it's a capacity problem.

And capacity is set by people who don't attend your retrospectives.

## Why This Keeps Happening

I asked that team with 47 abandoned action items what happened. Every answer was a variation of: "We didn't have time."

They had time. They had the same two weeks everyone else had. What they didn't have was permission to use it.

**Improvement work is invisible work:**

When you ship a feature, there's a demo. Someone says "nice." When you fix your flaky test suite, nobody notices except the team. Guess which one gets prioritized when sprint planning gets tight?

Stakeholders see velocity. They see burndown charts. They don't see "we reduced context-switching by consolidating our standups." That action item gets pushed to next sprint. Then the one after that. Then it dies in Jira, marked "won't do."

**The velocity trap:**

Most teams are planned to capacity. Every sprint is full before it starts. There's no slack for improvement work, and there's definitely no slack for dealing with the consequences of skipping improvement work.

This is a choice someone made. Not the team. The people who set the sprint commitments. The people who measure "productivity" by story points delivered. They optimized for output and sacrificed adaptability.

**The ownership gap:**

Product owns the product backlog. Nobody owns the improvement backlog.

Action items from retros go into a shared doc, or a separate Jira project, or nowhere. Nobody's job depends on them getting done. Nobody reports on them to leadership. They exist in a accountability vacuum.

**What actually works:**

The teams I've seen succeed at this do one of two things. Either they reserve 10-15% of sprint capacity for improvement work, explicitly, negotiated with stakeholders upfront. Or they make the improvement work visible by tying it to delivery: "We can't commit to this feature until we fix the deployment pipeline that failed three times last sprint."

Both require the Scrum Master to have a spine. And organizational air cover to use it.

## The Contrarian Take

Stop putting action items in your backlog. Put them in your Sprint Goal. Or accept they won't happen.
