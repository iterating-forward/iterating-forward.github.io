**Summary**: The PO role is treated as essential to Scrum. But the role only exists because Product, Project, and Engineering don't collaborate directly. Fix the collaboration, and the bottleneck disappears.

**Tech Context (Mar 2026)**: Scrum Guide Expansion Pack sparks renewed debate on team roles. "Product Developers" framing signals shift toward broader ownership. Companies continue trimming middle-layer roles.

---

Every burned-out Product Owner I've met has the same complaint: "I can't be in three places at once."

They're right. And that's the problem with the role.

## Three Jobs, One Calendar

The PO serves three masters every day.

**Product wants strategy:** What's the roadmap? Why this feature? Where are we going next quarter? What's the market saying?

**Project wants delivery:** When will this ship? Are we on track? What's the status for the steering committee? Can we commit to this date?

**Engineering wants execution:** What does this story mean? We hit an edge case — what do we do? Can you clarify acceptance criteria? Is this done enough?

Each group thinks the PO should prioritize them. The PO is always failing someone.

When they're in a roadmap meeting, engineers wait for answers.
When they're grooming backlog, stakeholders escalate about dates.
When they're firefighting scope creep, nobody's doing discovery.

## The Bottleneck Nobody Questions

Here's what bothers me: we designed this.

Product has questions about technical feasibility. Instead of asking Engineering directly, they ask the PO.

Engineering has questions about business priority. Instead of asking Product directly, they ask the PO.

Project needs a status update. Instead of looking at the board or asking the team, they ask the PO.

Everything routes through one person. Then we wonder why they're slow, overwhelmed, and making mistakes.

The PO role is a translation layer between groups that should be talking to each other.

## Why This Happened

Somewhere along the line, organizations decided that Product, Project, and Engineering couldn't collaborate without a mediator.

Maybe they had bad experiences. Product people who didn't respect technical constraints. Engineers who ignored business context. Project managers who set dates without asking anyone.

So they created a role to sit in the middle and absorb the friction. The PO became a buffer. A shock absorber. A single point of failure.

It worked, sort of. It also created a permanent bottleneck and let everyone else off the hook for collaborating.

## What High-Functioning Teams Do Instead

The best teams I've worked with don't have a traditional PO. They have:

**Product people who talk directly to engineers.** Not through tickets. Actual conversations about trade-offs, constraints, and "what if we did it this way instead?"

**Engineers who understand business context.** They know why features matter. They push back when scope doesn't make sense. They're in the room when priorities are set.

**Delivery tracking as a shared responsibility.** The board is public. Everyone knows the status. Nobody needs to ask a human for an update.

The work the PO does still happens. It's just distributed across people who are closer to the actual decisions.

## The Uncomfortable Implication

If your team can't function without a dedicated PO, the problem isn't missing headcount. The problem is that Product, Project, and Engineering have stopped collaborating.

The PO role is organizational scar tissue. It formed to protect dysfunctional relationships instead of fixing them.

Hiring another PO won't solve it. You'll just add another buffer between groups that should be working together.

## The Contrarian Take

You don't need a Product Owner. You need Product, Project, and Engineering to actually talk to each other. The PO role exists to avoid solving that harder problem.
