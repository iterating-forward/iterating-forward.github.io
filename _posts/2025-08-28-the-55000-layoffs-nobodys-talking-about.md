**Summary**: Mid-2025 data shows 55,000 layoffs directly attributed to AI. But the real story isn't the number - it's who's getting cut and why.

**Tech Context (Aug 2025)**: Challenger Gray & Christmas reports 55,000 AI-attributed layoffs in 2025. 40% of Microsoft developer layoffs. IBM CEO says it's "overhiring correction," not AI. 55% of employers regret AI layoffs.

---

If AI is so good at coding, why are the layoffs hitting QA, support, and middle management harder than developers?

## The Narrative vs. Reality

The "AI layoffs" narrative is convenient cover for workforce reduction that companies wanted to do anyway.

The IBM CEO admitted it: this is an overhiring correction from 2020-2023. AI is the excuse, not the cause.

Meanwhile, the actual developers using AI tools are more productive and more valuable than ever.

## What the Data Shows

Fifty-five thousand sounds like a lot. It's less than 1% of total job losses tracked by Challenger Gray in the same period. The "AI layoff wave" is a rounding error dressed up as a trend.

But the details are more interesting than the headline.

**Who actually got cut:**

Entry-level roles. Support staff. Project coordinators. QA testers. Middle managers whose job was "status aggregation." The pattern isn't "AI replaced technical work." The pattern is "AI gave cover for headcount reduction in roles that were already undervalued."

Microsoft cut 40% of a developer division and cited AI. Then rehired for senior roles three months later. IBM's CEO explicitly called it an overhiring correction from the pandemic boom. These weren't AI replacements. They were cost cuts that would've happened anyway.

**The regret data:**

Over half of employers who made AI-attributed layoffs regret them. They lost institutional knowledge. They created skill gaps. They discovered the people they cut were doing things they didn't realize needed doing.

One company I worked with laid off their entire documentation team because "AI can write docs now." Six months later, their support tickets doubled. The AI-generated docs were technically accurate and completely unusable.

**The skill gap paradox:**

The same companies citing AI for entry-level layoffs are complaining about senior talent shortages. You can't cut the pipeline and then wonder why you have no mid-levels in three years. But that's a future problem, and layoffs solve a quarterly earnings problem.

## The Contrarian Take

The people being laid off "for AI" are mostly not being replaced by AI. They're just not being replaced.
