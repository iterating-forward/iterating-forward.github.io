**Summary**: As AI accelerates development velocity, the story point debate resurfaces. Teams are asking if estimation still matters when AI can just... do the work.

**Tech Context (Nov 2025)**: GitHub Copilot surpasses 20 million users. Claude 4 and Opus 4 released - "world's best coding model." AI coding agents becoming mainstream in developer workflows.

---

A PM asked me last week if we should "re-estimate tickets after the AI writes them."

## The Misunderstanding

Story points were never meant to predict how long something takes.

They're a tool for conversation about:
- Complexity
- Risk
- Uncertainty

The teams using story points as time estimates are the same teams frustrated that AI "breaks" their velocity.

## What AI Revealed

That PM wanted to re-estimate after AI because the 8-point ticket got done in two hours instead of two days. Their velocity charts suddenly looked "wrong."

They weren't wrong. They were always meaningless. AI just made it obvious.

**The original sin:**

Somewhere along the line, teams started converting story points to hours. "An 8 is about two days." Managers started tracking velocity to predict delivery dates. Suddenly you had a relative sizing tool being used for absolute scheduling.

Story points were invented so teams could talk about "this feels bigger than that" without committing to deadlines. The moment you attach time expectations, you've rebuilt waterfall with different vocabulary.

**What AI exposed:**

A developer using Copilot finishes an 8-point ticket in two hours. The code works. The tests pass. By story point logic, nothing is wrong. The ticket was an 8 because it was complex, uncertain, or risky. The AI handled the complexity. The uncertainty resolved faster than expected. That's a good outcome.

But if you were secretly using that 8 to mean "two days of calendar time," now your sprint math is broken. You committed to 40 points thinking it meant two weeks of work. The team finished in four days. Do you pull in more work mid-sprint? Sit idle? Panic?

**What to do instead:**

Stop tracking velocity as a planning input. Use it as a health indicator. Stable velocity over time means the team has consistent capacity. Volatile velocity means something's off. But velocity doesn't tell you when features will ship, and it never did.

If you need predictability, use cycle time. Measure how long tickets actually take from start to done. That's empirical. That accounts for AI. That tells you something real about your delivery capability.

## The Contrarian Take

The best teams I've worked with stopped using story points years ago. Not because estimation is useless - because they found something that works better for them.
